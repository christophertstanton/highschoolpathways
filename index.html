Online Career Exploration & Assessment

Start here if you’re unsure what you want to do. These tools use your interests, aptitudes, and values to surface career ideas and the education or training that goes with them.

CareerOneStop – Interest & Skills Assessments

Run by the U.S. Department of Labor

CareerOneStop offers short, free assessments that ask about the kinds of activities you enjoy and the skills you want to use. You get a list of matching careers with typical wages, job outlook, and links to required education or training programs.

It’s a good starting point if you don’t have a clear target yet and want to see a broad range of options tied to real labor market data.

Best for: Students who want a quick, research-based snapshot of careers that fit their interests.

Visit CareerOneStop

BigFuture (College Board) – Careers & Multiple Pathways

Integrated with college & scholarship tools

BigFuture lets you take short quizzes to connect interests and strengths with career families. For each career, you can see what people do on the job, typical salary ranges, and the education required—from certificates and 2-year degrees to 4-year programs and beyond.

Because BigFuture also hosts college, scholarship, and technical program searches, you can move directly from “this sounds interesting” to “where could I study this and how might I pay for it?”

Best for: Students comparing multiple pathways (college, certificates, technical programs) side-by-side.

Explore careers on BigFuture

O*NET OnLine – Deep Dive into Occupations

Detailed data for 900+ U.S. occupations

O*NET is the federal government’s master database of jobs. Each occupation profile breaks down day-to-day tasks, required knowledge and skills, typical education levels, work styles, technology used, and wages.

Students can browse occupations by interest area (like “Helping People” or “Building & Fixing Things”), see related careers, and compare how much education each path usually requires.

Best for: Digging into the details of a specific job you’re already curious about.

Explore occupations on O*NET

YouScience – Aptitude-Based Career Discovery

Aptitudes + interests → best-fit careers

Unlike self-report surveys, YouScience uses interactive “brain games” to measure aptitudes such as spatial reasoning, numerical reasoning, and pattern recognition. Those aptitudes, combined with your interests, are matched to hundreds of careers and programs where people with similar profiles tend to thrive.

Many schools provide access through their counseling or CTE offices, giving students individualized reports they can discuss with counselors and families.

Best for: Students who like data-driven feedback about what they are naturally good at.

Learn about YouScience

My Next Move – Simple Career Explorer

Student-friendly interface from U.S. DOL

My Next Move is a streamlined site where you can search by keywords (“nurse,” “video games”), browse by industry, or take an “Interest Profiler” quiz. Each career page is written in plain language and shows typical tasks, necessary skills, education, salary, and job outlook.

It’s intentionally lightweight and visual, making it less overwhelming than a full database while still linking back to official labor-market information.

Best for: Quick exploration when you want straightforward, one-page snapshots of careers.

Visit My Next Move

Skill Building & Experiential Learning

These options let you try out fields, build real skills, and collect experiences that strengthen college, apprenticeship, or job applications.

Khan Academy & Coursera – Online Courses

Academic + career skills, mostly free

Khan Academy offers free, self-paced courses in math, science, economics, computer programming, and life skills. Their “Careers” video series lets you hear directly from people in different jobs about how they got there and what their work is like.

Coursera hosts university-level courses and career certificates in areas like data science, IT support, business, and health. Many can be audited for free, and some include beginner-friendly pathways for high schoolers.

Best for: Building concrete skills (coding, data, languages, business) and testing your interest in a subject before committing to a major.

Visit Khan Academy Visit Coursera

Roadtrip Nation – Stories & Project-Based Exploration

Video library + short course

Roadtrip Nation produces documentary-style videos of real people explaining how they found their paths—from traditional careers to unconventional jobs you might never have heard of. Their project-based course guides you through reflecting on your own interests, interviewing someone you admire, and mapping possible paths.

Many schools and nonprofits use Roadtrip Nation materials to make career exploration feel more human and less like filling out forms.

Best for: Students who learn best through stories and real-life examples, not just data tables.

Explore Roadtrip Nation

Skilled Trades & Apprenticeships

Interested in working with your hands, solving concrete problems, and earning while you learn? These resources focus specifically on skilled trades and registered apprenticeship programs.

High School CTE Programs

Hands-on training in high-demand fields

Career & Technical Education (CTE) programs in high schools and regional vocational centers offer courses plus lab or shop time in areas like health careers, IT, construction, manufacturing, culinary arts, and digital media. Many lead to industry certifications, college credit, or direct connections to apprenticeship programs.

Ask your counselor or district CTE office what pathways exist in your region and how they connect to local employers and apprenticeship options.

Best for: Students who want to graduate with both a diploma and job-ready skills in specific trades or technical fields.

mikeroweWORKS – Work Ethic Scholarships

Scholarships for trade & technical training

The mikeroweWORKS Foundation’s Work Ethic Scholarship helps students pay for training in high-demand skilled trades such as welding, HVAC, plumbing, electrical work, heavy equipment operation, and more. Applications emphasize work ethic, personal responsibility, and a commitment to hands-on careers.

Awards can help cover tuition, tools, and certification costs, reducing debt for students who choose trade programs over traditional four-year colleges.

Best for: Students committed to high-skill, high-demand trades who are ready to apply to an approved training program.

Explore mikeroweWORKS scholarships

Apprenticeship.gov – Earn While You Learn

Registered apprenticeships across many trades

Apprenticeship.gov is the federal portal for finding and understanding registered apprenticeships in fields like construction, advanced manufacturing, IT, transportation, and healthcare. As an apprentice, you are hired by an employer, receive structured on-the-job training plus related classroom instruction, and earn a wage that typically increases over time.

The site’s “Career Seekers” section explains how apprenticeships work, provides a searchable job finder, and links to local support organizations— including American Job Centers—that can help you apply.

Best for: Students who prefer learning by doing and want to earn money while gaining a recognized credential in a skilled trade.

Explore Apprenticeship.gov

Networking & Job Searching

These tools help you build a professional presence, find part-time jobs and internships, and learn what employers actually look for in early-career candidates.

LinkedIn – Professional Networking Profile

For students 16+ with parent/guardian guidance

LinkedIn is the standard platform for professional networking. High school students can create a basic profile highlighting coursework, projects, volunteer work, and extracurriculars, then connect with mentors, alumni, and local employers.

Many companies post internships, apprenticeships, and part-time roles on LinkedIn, and the platform’s “Alumni” and “People” search features help you see real paths taken by people in jobs you’re curious about.

Best for: Building a credible online presence and staying in touch with adult mentors you meet.

Go to LinkedIn

WayUp & Internship Platforms

Internships & entry-level roles

WayUp focuses on internships and early-career jobs, especially for college students and recent grads, and helps candidates get discovered through profile-based matching.

Other internship platforms (such as InternJobs or InternshipFinder) aggregate thousands of listings and often include guides on writing résumés, preparing for interviews, and converting internships into full-time offers.

Historically, Internships.com was a major internship search site; today, its role is largely filled by these newer platforms and by general job boards.

Best for: Students ready to apply for specific internships or part-time jobs.

Visit WayUp Explore InternshipFinder Explore InternJobs

American Job Centers

Free, in-person career help across the U.S.

American Job Centers (AJCs) provide “one-stop” local access to career counseling, job listings, résumé and interview workshops, and training referrals. Many centers have youth specialists who understand high school and post-secondary transitions.

AJCs can also connect you to apprenticeship opportunities, subsidized training, or local employers willing to hire young workers and provide on-the-job training.

Best for: Students who want face-to-face guidance or need help navigating financial aid and training programs.

School & Local Resources

Online tools are powerful, but they work best when combined with conversations and support from people who know your local context.

School Counselors

Your first stop for personalized guidance

School counselors can help you interpret assessment results, compare college and training options, and understand graduation requirements. They often know about local scholarships, bridge programs, dual-enrollment options, and support services you won’t discover online.

Bringing printed results from tools like CareerOneStop, My Next Move, or YouScience can make these conversations more concrete and action-oriented.

Best for: Turning online exploration into an actual plan for next year and beyond.

Local CTE & Dual-Enrollment Options

Combine high school with college or training

Many districts offer dual-enrollment (earning college credit in high school), early college, or partnerships with technical schools and community colleges. These can lower the cost and time required for degrees and certificates, including pathways aligned with skilled trades.

Your counselor or CTE coordinator can help you identify which programs you qualify for and how they connect to careers you’ve explored on this site.

Best for: Students who want to start earning college or technical credits before high school graduation.

Putting it all together

A practical sequence might look like: take an interest or aptitude assessment → explore matching careers on BigFuture, O*NET, or My Next Move → identify needed education or training → talk with your school counselor and local American Job Center → compare college, skilled trades, and apprenticeships → build skills and experience through online courses, CTE, YouthWorks (if you’re in MA), internships, or apprenticeships.
